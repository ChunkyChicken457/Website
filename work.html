The Foundation

Short Films Are Not
Small Films

A short film isn't simply a feature with scenes removed. It's a distinct art form with its own grammar — one that demands economy, precision, and a ruthless commitment to a single, focused idea. Where features sprawl, short films distill.

The best short films leave audiences with exactly one thing: a feeling that lingers. Not a dozen plot points, not five character arcs. One moment. One truth. One image they can't shake. Your script's entire job is to arrive at that moment as inevitably and efficiently as possible.

"A short film is not a story waiting to grow up — it is a complete thought, perfectly formed."

Restraint isn't a limitation. It's your greatest creative weapon. Learn to love the cut, the implication, the frame that does the work of a thousand words.

Core Principles

The Essential
Tips

07
01
Start With a Single, Undeniable Idea

A short film lives or dies by the strength of its core concept. Ask yourself: can you describe the idea in one sentence? If not, it's probably two ideas. Pick the more interesting one and cut everything else.

Concept
02
Arrive Late, Leave Early

Enter every scene as late as possible and exit before it's finished. Your audience is smart — they'll fill the gaps. Anticipation and aftermath are often more powerful than the event itself.

Pacing
03
Show the Inner Life Through Action

Your character's emotional state must live in behavior, not monologue. What someone does when no one is watching reveals everything. Replace exposition with a single telling gesture.

Character
04
Earn Your Ending — Don't Explain It

The ending must feel both surprising and inevitable. Avoid the impulse to explain what just happened through dialogue. If the image doesn't land on its own, the script isn't finished yet.

Resolution
05
Write to Your Resources

Two characters. One location. Daylight. These aren't constraints — they're creative parameters that separate real filmmakers from dreamers. The best short films in history were made with almost nothing.

Production
06
Subtext Is the Script

No character should say what they really mean. Ever. People don't talk in statements — they talk around the thing. What is unsaid creates tension; what is said releases it. Use this deliberately.

Dialogue
07
The Question Your Film Must Answer

Before writing page one, know what question your film asks — and what it answers. Not a theme stated in dialogue, but a dramatic question: "Will she say goodbye before it's too late?" Everything in the script should push toward or pull against that question's resolution.

The Foundation
Blueprint

The Three-Act
Short

Act I sets up the world and wound. Act II forces the confrontation. Act III pays off everything that came before — and nothing that didn't.

  • ACT I

    The Hook (Pages 1–2)

    Establish your character's world and wound in a single image or scene. We need to understand what they want — and what they're afraid of — before anything happens. Drop us into a moment already in motion.

  • ACT II

    The Obstacle (Pages 3–8)

    Introduce the event that forces your character to confront their wound. Each scene should raise the stakes or deepen the complication. The midpoint is the moment of no return — after this, going back is no longer possible.

  • ACT III

    The Reckoning (Pages 9–12)

    Your character makes a choice that costs them something real. The resolution doesn't need to be happy — it needs to be honest. The final image should echo the first, transformed by everything that came between.

  • ALWAYS

    The Title Card

    Your title is the first line of your screenplay. It sets tone, makes a promise, and should be deeply specific to your story. Avoid generic titles. Find the title that could only belong to your film.

Before You Write

The Pre-Writing
Checklist

  • Know your ending before you write your opening scene
  • Identify the single location that best externalizes your theme
  • Write a one-sentence logline — if it bores you, the idea does too
  • Know what changed in your character from page 1 to the last page
  • List every character — if any can be cut, cut them
  • Define your visual metaphor: the one image that carries your theme
  • Ask: would I watch this? Be brutally honest
What to Avoid

The Four Fatal
Mistakes

✕

The Twist Ending That Breaks Your Rules

A twist must recontextualize everything that came before — it cannot simply negate it. If your final revelation makes the rest of the story pointless, it's not a twist. It's a cheat. Audiences feel cheated even when they can't articulate why.

✕

Dialogue That Explains the Story

"I can't believe it's been three years since the accident" is not dialogue. It's an information delivery system wearing a human disguise. If a character is explaining the plot, you have a structural problem — not a dialogue problem.

✕

A Protagonist With No Want

Your main character must want something in every scene — not in a general life sense, but in a specific, immediate, cinematic sense. A character who simply reacts to events is not a protagonist. They're a witness.

✕

The Film That Runs Out of Story

Many short films run out of story at minute seven and coast to an ending. Every scene must earn its place. If you can cut a scene and the film makes more sense, cut the scene. Always cut the scene.

Going Deeper

Advanced Principles
Worth Internalizing

Visual Storytelling Over Dialogue

Cinema is a visual medium. Every scene should be comprehensible with the sound off. If your film only works with the dialogue, you've written a radio play. Ask: what does this scene look like?

The Character Arc Is Non-Negotiable

Even in 10 minutes, your protagonist must be different at the end. Not necessarily better — but changed. Something must have been learned, lost, or surrendered. Stasis is not a story.

Sound and Silence as Storytelling

Write sound into your script when it matters. A ticking clock. The specific silence after a door closes. Sound design begins on the page. A writer who understands the sonic texture of their world thinks cinematically.

Write the Script. Then Cut 20%.

Your first draft will always be longer than it should be. Draft one is about discovery. Draft two is about honesty. Cut every scene that doesn't advance or deepen. Then cut again. The film is in the cuts.

Location as Character

Where your story takes place should not be arbitrary. The location should actively pressure your characters or reveal their psychology. A story about grief set in a carnival is already interesting before a single line is written.

Watch 100 Short Films

No craft guide replaces firsthand study. Watch short films obsessively. Ask what each one is actually about — not in plot terms, but in emotional terms. Notice the structures, the silences. Learn by immersion.
